CGST vs SGST vs IGST: Key Differences

Published on January 2025 • 8 min read

Introduction

One of the most confusing aspects of GST for new business owners is understanding the three types of taxes: CGST, SGST, and IGST. While they seem complicated, the concept is actually straightforward once you understand how the Indian federal system works.

The Three Types of GST

1. CGST (Central Goods and Services Tax)

CGST is the GST collected by the Central Government. When you make a sale within your state, the Central Government collects a portion of the GST.

2. SGST (State Goods and Services Tax)

SGST is the GST collected by the State Government. When you make a sale within your state, the State Government collects its portion of the GST.

3. IGST (Integrated Goods and Services Tax)

IGST is the combined tax collected for inter-state transactions. When you sell to another state, the entire GST is collected by the Central Government as IGST.

When to Use Which Tax?

Use CGST + SGST When:

  • Seller and buyer are in the same state
  • You're selling goods or services within your state
  • This is called Intra-State Supply
  • Example: A Delhi business selling to another Delhi business

Use IGST When:

  • Seller and buyer are in different states
  • You're selling goods or services to another state
  • This is called Inter-State Supply
  • Example: A Delhi business selling to a Mumbai business

How CGST and SGST Split the Tax

In intra-state transactions, the GST rate is split equally between CGST and SGST:

  • 5% GST rate: 2.5% CGST + 2.5% SGST
  • 12% GST rate: 6% CGST + 6% SGST
  • 18% GST rate: 9% CGST + 9% SGST
  • 28% GST rate: 14% CGST + 14% SGST

Practical Examples

Example 1: Intra-State Supply (CGST + SGST)

Scenario: A fabric shop in Delhi sells cotton cloth worth ₹1,000 to a tailor in Delhi. GST rate is 12%.

  • Base Amount: ₹1,000
  • CGST (6%): ₹60 (goes to Central Government)
  • SGST (6%): ₹60 (goes to Delhi State Government)
  • Total GST: ₹120
  • Customer pays: ₹1,120

Example 2: Inter-State Supply (IGST)

Scenario: The same Delhi fabric shop sells the same ₹1,000 cloth to a tailor in Mumbai. GST rate is 12%.

  • Base Amount: ₹1,000
  • IGST (12%): ₹120 (goes to Central Government)
  • No CGST or SGST
  • Customer pays: ₹1,120

GST Collection and Remittance

For Intra-State Sales:

As a seller, you collect both CGST and SGST from your customer. At the time of filing GSTR-1 (monthly return), you report:

  • Total CGST collected (which you'll eventually remit to Central Government)
  • Total SGST collected (which you'll eventually remit to State Government)

For Inter-State Sales:

You collect only IGST from the customer, which is remitted entirely to the Central Government.

GST Refunds and Input Tax Credit

How Input Tax Credit Works

One of the key benefits of GST is Input Tax Credit (ITC). If you purchase goods/services with GST, you can claim the GST paid as credit.

ITC for Intra-State Purchases:

If you purchase goods in your state, you claim both CGST and SGST as credit.

ITC for Inter-State Purchases:

If you purchase goods from another state, you claim IGST as credit.

Small Example with ITC

Scenario: Delhi retailer buys ₹1,000 cloth (12% GST) and sells for ₹2,000 (12% GST). Same state transaction.

  • Purchase: ₹1,000 + ₹120 GST (₹60 CGST + ₹60 SGST) = ₹1,120
  • Sale: ₹2,000 + ₹240 GST (₹120 CGST + ₹120 SGST) = ₹2,240
  • GST Payable = GST Collected - GST Paid = ₹240 - ₹120 = ₹120
  • CGST Payable: ₹120 - ₹60 = ₹60
  • SGST Payable: ₹120 - ₹60 = ₹60

Key Differences Summary Table

Feature CGST/SGST IGST
When Used Intra-state (same state) Inter-state (different states)
Tax Distribution Split between Central & State All to Central Government
Number of Taxes Two (CGST + SGST) One (IGST)
Rate Split 50% each 100% combined

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing intra-state with inter-state transactions
  • Using IGST for same-state supplies
  • Incorrectly calculating CGST and SGST split
  • Forgetting to adjust for ITC when calculating final GST payable
  • Not checking buyer's state before determining which tax to apply

Conclusion
